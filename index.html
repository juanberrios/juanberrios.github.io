Profile

I am currently a second-year PhD student at the Department of Linguistics of the University of Pittsburgh. I am primarily interested in morphosyntactic variation in both native and learner grammars. With this goal in mind, in my research I consider the influence of linguistic, social, and longitudinal factors on variation and its acquisition, with an emphasis on usage-based explanations. Likewise, I employ sociolinguistic, computational, and corpus-based methods for data collection and analysis. In my role as a teaching fellow, I am also involved in the design of assessments, instructional materials, and curricula for Spanish-language courses.

Recent work

Diminutive suffix productivity

A cross-dialectal analysis of the productivity (i.e., the extent to which a morphological pattern can be applied to new bases to form new words) of two competing Spanish diminutive suffixes (-ito, -illo). The most recent version of this project, with a focus on the theoretical underpinnings, was presented at Georgetown University's 2020 Graduate Portuguese and Hispanic Symposium.

Variable adjective position in Venezuelan Spanish

We examine variability in the position of attributive adjectives in Spanish through a corpus-based variationist study. Results indicate that syllabic length differences, lexical frequency (both absolute and relative), and semantic class play a role on variable adjective ordering in Venezuelan Spanish. The most recent version of this project was presented at the Hispanic Linguistics Symposium 2019.
